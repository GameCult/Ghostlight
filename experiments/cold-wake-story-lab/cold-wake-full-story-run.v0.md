## Story Draft

Sella's board did not soften after she spoke. It became more specific.

That was how the door opened in Aya space: not by mercy defeating procedure, but by procedure finally admitting it had a pulse. The cold repair bay stayed blue on the clinic wall, not green. Blue meant touched, not taken in. Blue meant the staff could put hands, sensors, seals, and heat loops against a problem without promising the whole sanctuary would become its body.

Across the wet/dry work surface, Maer's interface answered her.

The pool-side acoustic field brightened first, a low shimmer through the water where a human eye would have expected a status light. Then the dryside board translated it into lines Sella's staff could audit: overtime, heat-management exposure, decon route Three, isolation failure, failed extraction. Maer's ledger seal re-locked beside each cost and extended into a live band tied to the countdown.

No one in the bay cheered. That would have been vulgar. Also inaccurate.

"Accepted," Maer said through the shared frame. "Bounded diagnostic contact only. No full intake, no custody, no personhood finding, no cargo designation. Packet sound remains preservation-only until diagnostic handling is complete. Decon route Three. Isolation absolute. My ledger stays live for staff time, heat burden, and failed extraction cost. Permission is not capacity; the ledger will not pretend otherwise. Open the channel on your timing mark, Sella. We will listen before the window closes."

The words were careful enough to satisfy the permit shell and heavy enough not to sound like obedience. Sella watched the ledger take the burden. It did not make air. It did not conjure rested hands from exhausted ones. It did, at least, stop pretending the bill belonged to the nearest person kind enough to notice it.

The preservation channel opened as a thin acoustic lane, diagnostic, noncustodial, nonorigin, unresolved. Outside the clinic's sealed walls, the contact craft held at decon route Three and waited for Sella's mark.

For a few seconds the whole room listened to nothing.

That was the shape of Cold Wake: not an explosion, not a heroic boarding burn, not even a voice. Just a quiet-running vessel somewhere past its own safe heat, a room full of people trying to touch it without lying about what touch would cost, and a clock that did not care what any of them meant.

Sella let the room move, but only by inches. The diagnostic sleeve woke amber, not green, because green was how institutions lied to themselves about capacity.

"Proceed through Three on my mark," she said. "Diagnostic sleeve only. No intake language, no custody drift, no ancestry fishing, no testimony conversion. The sound is preserved, not interpreted. Staff pair, stay outside the sleeve unless I name a breach. Maer, your ledger is sufficient for contact, not sufficient for a miracle. We listen, we handle heat, we keep isolation, and nobody turns motion into permission after the fact."

The craft moved. Somewhere beyond the clinic, something too cold for comfort came close enough to be heard.
