# The Narrowest Possible Margin

The relay tribunal chamber over Ganymede had been built to make panic feel rude.

Its walls were pale mineral composite, its benches arranged in clean descending arcs, its convoy board set above the adjudicators like a second ceiling. On ordinary days the board showed traffic in patient greens and procedural amber. On this day, during the part of the Cold Wake Panic that later histories would file under *classification failure*, one point near the Jovian corridor pulsed in a color nobody in the room wanted to inherit.

No one called it a ship without qualification.

No one called it a life.

The quiet-running vessel had entered the corridor edge under contradictory thermal posture: cold where a ship should have been bleeding heat, noisy where certified stealth should have been clean, polite in the wrong fields and silent in the ones law had learned to trust. The PSC tribunal had done what tribunals did when reality arrived without its papers. It froze the category and called that restraint.

Isdra Vel stood below the board with her hands folded behind her back. Her voice could have cut glass if glass had first submitted a compliance schedule.

"The conditional hold stands, and a review timer is now active to evaluate whether the vessel's telemetry can clear for limited rescue contact without altering the risk category. We proceed on the narrowest possible margin until the data reconciles or the hold is formally lifted."

The room accepted this the way rooms accept weather. A few insurer clerks lowered their eyes to their pads. Two Navigator observers went very still.

Maer Tidecall did not move at first. He had the stillness of someone trained not to waste motion near a failing seal. The live board counted down review minutes in a corner of the display, each pulse a small official mercy.

Then he stood.

He was not tall in the dramatic way, and did not have the tribunal voice that made walls feel useful. He spoke as if the chamber itself had memory and could be ashamed of what it recorded.

Maer stands still as the board ticks, his voice a low, rhythmic counterpoint to the silence. "Record the cost of this delay in full; do not let the ledger balance itself against the lives held within."

Isdra looked at him as if he had put a knife on the table and called it arithmetic.

The board continued its soft work. Outside the chamber, the corridor traffic adjusted around the frozen point, giving it distance. The oldest fear in route law had always been contagious uncertainty: a signal you could not classify, a hull you could not price, a rescue you could not justify until the thing needing rescue had become easier to describe.

The review timer began.

By the time Maer reached the intake clinic behind the dockside pumpworks, Ganymede's public corridors had already learned the shape of the tribunal's caution. People near the pumpworks did not speak of the vessel directly. They spoke of dump windows, of undeclared actors, of pirates pretending to be refugees and refugees being mistaken for pirates because misery rarely filed clean manifests. They spoke of Cold Wake as if the phrase itself were a room one entered quietly.

The clinic had no room for quiet.

Aya networks did not make sanctuaries out of softness. They made them out of air scrubbers, legal shields, thermal blankets, repair printers, painkillers, stubborn staff, and the terrifying moral discipline of counting what could actually be done. Behind the pumpworks, the air tasted of iodine and hot metal. The triage board had more red on it than Maer wanted to see.

Sella Ren did not look away from the board when he entered.

"I know," she said before he could begin.

That was worse than refusal. Refusal would have given him a wall. Knowledge gave him a bill.

He told her what the tribunal had not cleared, what the vessel had sent, what the packet might be. Medical telemetry. Firmware noise. Something voice-like, if the receiver was kind or tired enough. He did not say testimony. He had learned, already, how a word could steal a fact before the fact had a chance to stand.

Sella kept her hands on the triage board, tapping the row of claimed beds before pointing to the single empty cot by the pump vibration. "Maer, I'll hold a conditional slot for your vessel, but you name exactly what packet you're displacing and which claimant I must turn away to make room." She waits for his specification, ensuring the rescue doesn't come at the cost of someone already inside the clinic.

There it was: not cruelty, not reluctance, not the little institutional shrug that let a person die in a side column. A real yes, with teeth.

Maer stepped out of the triage lane. A technician pushed past with a sealed tray of printed arterial mesh, and he let the motion interrupt him rather than forcing the clinic to arrange itself around his urgency.

"Please, let me speak plainly about the beds we are displacing rather than letting the tribunal's uncertainty become a moral burden for you alone. If we can define what support would make a conditional slot real without sacrificing those who already need care, I will ensure that weight is carried on the record, not on your conscience."

Sella's mouth tightened. Not a smile. Not forgiveness. An adjustment.

"Then start with oxygen-hours," she said, and handed him a slate.

The route from Ganymede to Callisto took less time than the tribunal review took to pretend it was not becoming a decision. Maer spent the transit with Sella's oxygen-hours on one slate and the vessel's corrupted packet on another. The packet did not resolve when stared at. It repeated product-like routing terms around a pattern that made his hindbrain reach for voice and his discipline refuse the word.

The Callisto spillover office was leased under a vendor name that had never manufactured anything heavy enough to cast a useful shadow. Its reception wall displayed certifications from Rossum, Cryonix, and three shell entities whose logos had the expensive humility of things designed to survive subpoenas. Veyr Oss waited behind a terminal that reflected Maer's face back at him as a legal inconvenience.

Veyr was polite before he was human. That was not an accusation; it was the order of operations.

"Route Steward," he said. "The artifact is derivative firmware associated with a sealed routing chain. I can provide compliance posture, not metaphysics."

Maer watched Veyr's hand near the close command. The terminal offered no drama. Drama was what people invented after a thing had already been done.

Maer places a steady hand on the terminal's edge to halt the closing command, his voice low and ritual-calm. "You will not let the chain dissolve before we name its custody; state the claimant-status flag or I will freeze this interface until the rescue ledger demands it."

Veyr did not flinch. He had probably trained that out of himself at considerable expense.

But his hand withdrew.

Veyr withdraws his hand from the terminal and slides a narrow preservation hash across the interface, redirecting the claimant-status into a technical flag while insisting the routing artifact remains a sealed vendor chain until full custody escrow is verified.

"A preservation hash," Maer said.

"A compliance artifact," Veyr corrected.

"A thing you are suddenly willing to preserve."

Veyr's eyes flicked, once, toward the tribunal timer in the corner of the feed. "Preservation is not admission."

"No," Maer said. "But deletion would have been."

He took the flag and left before the office could become an argument about whether moral facts existed inside product language. He did not have time to win that argument. The vessel was still cold in the wrong places and hot where the law did not want to look.

At the convoy threshold, Jupiter filled the far display like a god too large to bother judging anyone. The vessel's heat-debt window had fallen into minutes. Around it, legitimate traffic held formation with the bitter patience of captains watching another captain become a lesson.

Isdra was back on the tribunal feed. Her face had not changed. That was part of the craft: to let the situation deteriorate while the face remained eligible for office.

Maer sent the preservation hash, the claimant-status flag, Sella's oxygen-hours, and the review timer's failure to become knowledge. He did not send certainty. Certainty would have been easier. Certainty would have been a lie with good posture.

"Isdra, the heat-debt window closes before the tribunal can freeze us, so I offer you this procedural path: take the custody flag and issue a limited contact order that preserves the vessel's life and evidence without breaking the table. This narrow route allows you to bend the freeze while keeping the institution whole."

For a moment, no one in the feed spoke.

The convoy board did its small violence. Seconds became records. Records became responsibility.

Isdra looked down, not away. Her fingers moved across an authorization pane with the kind of precision people use when they are cutting only the wire they can name.

"I am entering a provisional order limiting interaction to telemetry-preserving heat management and evidence preservation, effective until the tribunal resolves the vessel's provenance. This narrow exception maintains the freeze technically intact while preventing immediate loss of life, and any contact is strictly reversible upon confirmation of the threat."

The order propagated through the convoy net.

No one cheered. Cheering would have misunderstood the hour. A rescue contact launched under restriction, watched by insurers, logged by Navigators, received by a clinic that had already priced its mercy in oxygen-hours. The vessel answered with a telemetry burst that still did not say what it was.

But it answered.

Maer closed his eyes for the length of one breath. Somewhere behind Ganymede's pumpworks, Sella would be rearranging suffering with both hands. Somewhere in a Callisto office, Veyr Oss would be writing a report in which nothing important had happened in morally legible language. Isdra would preserve the freeze in the minutes, the exception in the order, and her fear in neither.

The corridor would remember the shape of it badly at first. Corridors always did. Some would say the tribunal had blinked. Some would say the Navigators had forced an exception. Some would say Aya had once again spent resources as if compassion were a solvent for math. Some would say Cognitum's product chains had learned a new way to hide.

The ledger would not settle the argument.

It would only record that, for one narrow margin, the law had been made to carry the cost of waiting where everyone could see it.
